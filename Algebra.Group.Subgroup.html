Subgroups🔗

A subgroup mmm of a group GGG is a monomorphism H→mGH \xto{m} GHm​G, that is, an object of the poset of subobjects Sub⁡(G)\Sub(G)Sub(G). Since group homomorphisms are injective exactly when their underlying function is an embedding, we can alternatively describe this as a condition on a predicate G→PropG \to \propG→Prop.

Subgroup : Group ℓ → Type (lsuc ℓ)
Subgroup {ℓ = ℓ} G = Precategory.Ob (Subobj (Groups ℓ) G)

A proposition H:G→PropH : G \to \propH:G→Prop of a group (G,⋆)(G, \star)(G,⋆) represents a subgroup if it contains the group unit, is closed under multiplication, and is closed under inverses.

record represents-subgroup (G : Group ℓ) (H : ℙ (G .fst)) : Type ℓ where
  open Group-on (G .snd)

  field
    has-unit : unit ∈ H
    has-⋆    : ∀ {x y} → x ∈ H → y ∈ H → (x ⋆ y) ∈ H
    has-inv  : ∀ {x} → x ∈ H → x ⁻¹ ∈ H

If HHH represents a subgroup, then its total space ΣH\Sigma HΣH inherits a group structure from GGG, and the first projection ΣH→G\Sigma H \to GΣH→G is a group homormophism.

rep-subgroup→group-on
  : (H : ℙ (G .fst)) → represents-subgroup G H → Group-on (Σ[ x ∈ G .fst ] x ∈ H)
rep-subgroup→group-on {G = G} H sg =
  make-group
    (Σ-is-hlevel 2 has-is-set λ x → is-prop→is-set (H x .is-tr))
    (unit , has-unit)
    (λ { (x , xin) (y , yin) → x ⋆ y , has-⋆ xin yin} )
    (λ { (x , xin) → (x ⁻¹ , has-inv xin) })
    (λ x y z → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) (sym associative))
    (λ x → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) inversel)
    (λ x → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) inverser)
    (λ x → Σ-prop-path (λ x → H x .is-tr) idl)
  where open Group-on (G .snd)
        open represents-subgroup sg

predicate→subgroup : (H : ℙ (G .fst)) → represents-subgroup G H → Subgroup G
predicate→subgroup {G = G} H p = cut map , ism where
  map : Groups.Hom (Σ (∣_∣ ⊙ H) , rep-subgroup→group-on H p) G
  map .fst = fst
  map .snd .Group-hom.pres-⋆ x y = refl

  ism : Groups.is-monic map
  ism g h p = Forget-is-faithful
    (embedding→monic (Subset-proj-embedding (is-tr ⊙ H)) (fst g) (fst h) (ap fst p))

Kernels and Images🔗

To a group homomorphism f:A→Bf : A \to Bf:A→B we can associate two canonical subgroups, one of AAA and one of BBB: fff’s image, written im⁡f\im fimf, is the subgroup of BBB “reachable by mapping through fff”, and fff’s kernel, written ker⁡f\ker fkerf, is the subgroup of AAA which fff sends to the unit.

The kernel can be cheapily described as a limit: It is the equaliser of fff and the zero morphism — which, recall, is the unique map A→BA \to BA→B which breaks down as A→0→BA \to 0 \to BA→0→B.

module _ {ℓ} where
  open Canonical-kernels (Groups ℓ) ∅ᴳ Groups-equalisers public

  Ker-subgroup : ∀ {A B : Group ℓ} → Groups.Hom A B → Subgroup A
  Ker-subgroup f = cut kernel , Groups.is-equaliser→is-monic _ has-is-kernel where
    open Kernel (Ker f)

Every group homomorphism f:A→Bf : A \to Bf:A→B has an image im⁡f\im fimf, defined by equipping the image with a group structure inherited from BBB. More concretely, we can describe the elements of im⁡f\im fimf as the “mere fibres” of fff: They consist of a point y:By : By:B, together with (the truncation of) a fibre of fff over yyy. We multiply xxx (in the fibre over aaa) with yyy (in the fibre over bbb), giving the element xyxyxy in the fibre over ababab.

For reasons that will become clear later, we denote the image of fff, when regarded as its own group, by A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f), and reserve the notation im⁡f\im fimf for that group regarded as a subgroup of BBB.

The construction of a group structure on A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) is unsurprising, so we leave it in this <details> tag for the curious reader. 
  A/ker[_] : Group ℓ
  A/ker[_] = T , grp where
    unit : T
    unit = B.unit , inc (A.unit , f.pres-id)

    inv : T → T
    inv (x , p) = x B.⁻¹ ,
      ∥-∥-map (λ { (y , p) → y A.⁻¹ , f.pres-inv _ ∙ ap B._⁻¹ p }) p

    mul : T → T → T
    mul (x , xp) (y , yp) = x B.⋆ y ,
      ∥-∥-elim₂ (λ _ _ → squash)
        (λ { (x* , xp) (y* , yp)
           → inc (x* A.⋆ y* , f.pres-⋆ _ _ ∙ ap₂ B._⋆_ xp yp) })
        xp yp

    grp : Group-on T
    grp = make-group Tset unit mul inv
      (λ x y z → Tpath (sym B.associative))
      (λ x → Tpath B.inversel) (λ x → Tpath B.inverser) λ x → Tpath B.idl

That the canonical inclusion map A/ker⁡(f)↪BA/\ker(f) \mono BA/ker(f)↪B deserves the name “image” comes from fff breaking down as a (regular) epimorphism into im⁡f\im fimf (written A→im), followed by that map:

(A→fB)=(A↠fA/ker⁡(f)↪B) (A \xto{f} B) = (A \xepi{f} A/\ker(f) \mono B) (Af​B)=(AfA/ker(f)↪B)

  A→im : Groups.Hom A A/ker[_]
  A→im .fst x = f .fst x , inc (x , refl)
  A→im .snd .Group-hom.pres-⋆ x y = Tpath (f.pres-⋆ _ _)

  im→B : Groups.Hom A/ker[_] B
  im→B .fst (b , _) = b
  im→B .snd .Group-hom.pres-⋆ x y = refl

When this monomorphism is taken as primary, we refer to A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) as im⁡f\im fimf.

  Im[_] : Subgroup B
  Im[_] = cut im→B , im↪B where
    im↪B : Groups.is-monic im→B
    im↪B = injective-group-hom im→B Tpath

The reason for denoting the set-theoretic image of f:A→Bf : A \to Bf:A→B (which is a subobject of BBB, equipped with BBB’s group operation) by A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) is the first isomorphism theorem (though we phrase it more categorically): The image of fff serves as a quotient for (the congruence generated by) ker⁡f\ker fkerf.

Note: In more classical texts, the first isomorphism theorem is phrased in terms of two pre-existing objects A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) (defined as the set of cosets of ker⁡(f)\ker(f)ker(f) regarded as a subgroup) and im⁡f\im fimf (defined as above). Here we have opted for a more categorical phrasing of that theorem: We know what the universal property of A/ker⁡(f)A/\ker(f)A/ker(f) is — namely that it is a specific colimit — so the specific construction used to implement it does not matter.

  1st-iso-theorem : Groups.is-coequaliser (Groups.Zero.zero→ ∅ᴳ) Kerf.kernel A→im
